# The Hype Tax: Soccer Clubs Are Overpaying Millions for Instagram Followers

Here's something wild I found when I started digging into soccer transfer data: the more Instagram followers a player has, the more likely their club overpaid for them.

I call it the Hype Tax.

I built a valuation model that estimates what a player should be worth based purely on what they do on the pitch — things like Expected Possession Value (EPV), progressive carries, pressing intensity, and chance creation. Then I compared those "fair values" to what the market actually charges.

The gap was massive.

**The Players Getting Overpaid**

Jude Bellingham (42 million followers) is overvalued by about 17%. Lamine Yamal (28.5 million followers) — also overvalued by 17%. Endrick (18.9 million) — overvalued by nearly 25%.

These are incredible players. Nobody's saying they're bad. But the data suggests clubs are paying a premium for fame that goes beyond on-pitch production.

Meanwhile, players like Adam Wharton (2.8 million followers) are undervalued by a staggering 121%. Johan Bakayoko (3.5 million followers) — undervalued by 55%. Evan Ferguson (3.8 million followers) — undervalued by 92%.

See the pattern? Low follower count, high on-pitch impact, cheap price tag.

**Why This Happens**

Soccer's transfer market has the same problem as the stock market: people confuse popularity with value. A player who scores a viral bicycle kick gets a market premium. A midfielder who quietly moves the ball into dangerous positions 80 times a game doesn't trend on TikTok — but they might be more valuable to winning.

**The Moneyball Opportunity**

If a club spent €200 million on the five most undervalued players in my analysis, they'd get roughly €317 million worth of on-pitch production. That's a 67% return on investment.

This is exactly the logic that powered Moneyball in baseball — finding value where the market isn't looking.

**The Question Nobody's Asking**

If social media following inflates transfer prices, does it also inflate wages? Are clubs paying higher salaries to popular players because they "need" to match the player's perceived status? And if so, does that mean the smartest clubs aren't the ones with the biggest budgets — they're the ones who ignore the hype?
